Training strategies by run type
‚ñæ

Running improvement doesn‚Äôt come from pushing hard every day. It comes from doing the right type of run at the right effort.

Easy runs should feel slow and relaxed. You should be able to talk in full sentences. These runs build your aerobic system and protect you from injury.

Recovery runs are even easier than easy. Their only goal is to help you feel better and recover from harder workouts.

Long runs train endurance and mental patience. They should feel controlled, not heroic. Practice fueling and hydration here.

Speed workouts improve efficiency and leg turnover. They should feel challenging but controlled, never like an all-out sprint.

Tempo runs teach you how to stay uncomfortable without panicking. This skill is essential on race day.

Hill workouts naturally improve strength and running form.

Pacing, cadence, stride length & heart rate
‚ñæ

Pacing

Pace is simply how fast you‚Äôre running. Beginners often start too fast and slow down later. Good pacing feels almost too easy at the start.

Cadence

Cadence is how many steps you take per minute. A slightly quicker cadence usually means shorter steps and less impact. Focus on light, quick steps ‚Äî not chasing a specific number.

Stride length

Stride length is how far each step travels. Overstriding (reaching too far forward) often causes injuries. Speed should come from pushing back, not reaching forward.

Heart rate

Heart rate shows internal effort. On easy runs, your heart rate should stay relatively low. If heart rate climbs, slow down ‚Äî even if the pace feels slow.
